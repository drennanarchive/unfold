1. Goal

DESTINATION

A goal is the desired future state or strategic outcome. It explains the direction of travel without jumping into the work plan.

Learn the distinction

  • Good goals are broad enough to matter.
  • They are clear enough to guide choices.
  • They are not tasks, meetings, products, or deliverables.
Goal = desired future state + strategic reason

Notes to carry forward

Readiness diagnostic

Can the team describe the desired future state in plain language?

Can the team explain why this matters strategically?

Is the statement free from task-list language?

Assess whether the team understands the goal layer.

2. Objective

NEXT OUTCOME

An objective is a specific outcome that advances the goal. It is not merely a sub-goal, and it is not a task.

Learn the distinction

  • Objectives narrow the goal into a specific outcome.
  • They should be scoped enough to plan against.
  • They should describe what will be different, not what people will do.
Objective = specific outcome that advances the goal

Notes to carry forward

Readiness diagnostic

Can the team name the specific outcome this effort should produce?

Can the team describe what NOT achieving it would look like?

Is it clearly different from a task, activity, or deliverable?

Assess whether the team understands the objective layer.

3. Key Result

PROOF OF CHANGE

A key result is a measurable outcome proving the objective was met. It measures change, not activity, and can be scored on a spectrum.

Learn the distinction

  • Key Results need a baseline and target.
  • They measure changed outcomes, not completed work.
  • A hard 70% may be better than an easy 100%.
Key Result = baseline → target + time box

Notes to carry forward

Readiness diagnostic

Can the team identify a measurable change, not just an activity?

Does the team know or know how to find the baseline?

Would the target demonstrate meaningful progress rather than easy completion?

Assess whether the team understands the Key Result layer.

4. Milestone

PROGRESS MARKER

A milestone is a binary event marking progress. It happened or it did not. It supports schedule awareness but does not prove the objective succeeded by itself.

Learn the distinction

  • Milestones are observable events.
  • They should be binary, dated, or sequenced.
  • They are not percentages or health metrics.
Milestone = binary event + date / sequence

Notes to carry forward

Readiness diagnostic

Can the team tell whether each milestone happened without debating percentages?

Are the likely checkpoints tied to sequence, timing, or dependency?

Does the team understand milestones are progress markers, not success metrics?

Assess whether the team understands the Milestone layer.

5. KPI

HEALTH SIGNAL

A KPI monitors ongoing operational health. Key Results measure change; KPIs monitor whether the system stays healthy over time.

Learn the distinction

  • KPIs are tracked continuously.
  • They signal health, risk, or degradation.
  • Someone should act if a KPI turns red.
KPI = ongoing health signal + cadence + owner

Notes to carry forward

Readiness diagnostic

Can the team distinguish the KPI from the Key Result?

Would this be monitored continuously after the KR period?

Can someone act if this turns red?

Assess whether the team understands the KPI layer.
